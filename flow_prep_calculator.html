Staining volume
The staining volume is how much liquid is in each tube when your cells are sitting with the antibodies. Standard is 100 µL — that's what datasheets are calibrated to. Your cells get resuspended directly in this volume (buffer + antibodies mixed together). The number below is how much total antibody cocktail you need to prepare.
Volume per tube (µL)
Standard is 100 µL. Don't change this unless you have a reason.
Total cocktail to prepare (µL)
= effective tubes × vol/tube. Make this much antibody mix in total.
Example: 12 samples × 1.1 overage = 14 effective tubes × 100 µL = 1400 µL of cocktail. You pipette 100 µL into each of the 12 tubes.
Washing
After incubating with antibodies, you wash the cells to remove any antibody that didn't bind. You add a large volume of the same PBS or FACS buffer you always use — nothing special — then centrifuge and aspirate. This step removes background signal. For surface staining, 1 wash is standard.

Final resuspension before acquisition
After washing, your cells are in a dry pellet. You resuspend them in buffer to put them back into suspension for the cytometer. DAPI goes into this same buffer — it is not a separate solution. You just add DAPI to your regular PBS/FACS buffer and use that. DAPI stains dead cells so you can exclude them when you gate.

Why dilute at all?
Most datasheets say 1–2 µL per sample. Pipetting 1 µL is unreliable — a tiny wobble in your hand is a 20% error. The fix: dilute your antibody first into a fresh tube, then pipette a larger volume (5 µL) that carries the exact same amount of antibody. Same concentration in the tube, much more accurate pipetting.

If an antibody already calls for ≥ 5 µL per sample, you don't need to dilute — just pull straight from the vial.
Target pipetting volume (µL)
What you want to pipette per sample. 5 µL is a good, accurate volume.
Dead volume buffer (µL)
Extra to prepare per Ab so you don't run out from tip losses. 20 µL is safe.

FMO tubes
An FMO tube contains all antibodies except the one you're gating on. It shows you where the true negative sits for that channel when all other fluorochromes are present (which spill into each other). Treat every FMO tube identically to your samples — same incubation, same wash, same DAPI.
